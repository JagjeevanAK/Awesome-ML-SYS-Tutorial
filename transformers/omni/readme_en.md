## Architecture concepts

### codec token

Codec is the abbreviation composed of the first letters of Compressor and Decompressor, and assumes the functions of encoding and decoding at the same time. In the encode stage, the audio encoder converts the original audio waveform (analog signal or high sampling rate digital signal) into discrete Tokens. In the decode stage, Talker will generate codec tokens based on the preamble information, and then restore the codec tokens back to sound waveforms through the audio decoder. Through neural network compression, Codec can compress high-frequency sampled audio signals into a very small amount of Tokens without losing almost any sound quality.

### Mainstream audio model architecture

1. Audio Encoding: Input the high-frequency original audio waveform to Audio Encoder to obtain codec tokens. Different from the traditional speech-to-text method (TTS/ASR), Audio Encoder retains information far beyond the text itself, such as the speaker's timbre, speaking speed, emotion and even background noise.

2. Understanding and reasoning (Thinker Decode): codec tokens will be passed to Thinker, a component of the transformer architecture, responsible for understanding and reasoning. Thinker prefills codec tokens to obtain last hidden states, and then further samples to obtain text tokens.

3. Audio encoding (Talker Decode): Talker receives text tokens and Hidden States from Tinker and generates codec tokens.

4. Audio Decoding: Input the codec tokens into the Audio Decoder to obtain the audio waveform (Waveform).### Why does Talker need to receive Thinker’s Hidden States?

This is the question I immediately had after understanding the Omni model, "Why does Talker need to receive Thinker's Last Hidden States?" In other words, "Since we have accepted Last Hidden States, why do we still need to accept text tokens?". Logically speaking, Last Hidden States should already contain text tokens information, which is enough to support Talker to generate codec tokens. With this question in mind, I searched for several answers. One of the points that I am more convinced of is the consistency between text output and audio output. From the perspective of product performance, we hope that users can also see subtitles while hearing sounds using the Omni model. If we let Talker start decoding codec tokens without waiting for the text tokens generated by Thinker, it will be technically difficult to make the codec tokens sampled by Talker display the same text as the text tokens sampled by Thinker. For users, the slight difference between voice and text output is acceptable, similar to hearing "I strongly agree", but seeing the text output is "I strongly agree", which is not harmful. However, the output is getting longer and longer, and this inconsistency will have a non-negligible impact on the user experience.

However, this does not convince me why Talker must wait for Thinker to generate text tokens before starting to decode codec tokens. An unproven idea quickly sprouted in my mind, let Talker and Thinker overlap decoding. Specifically, the current reasoning process is:

The voice encoder generates input codec tokens and passes them to thinker. Thinker completes prefill, gets the last hidden states, and then continues decoding to get all text tokens. Then it inputs the last hidden states and text tokens to talker, and talker decodes to get the output codec tokens, which are sent to the Codec Decoder, allowing the decoder to restore the final audio.

The reasoning process I envision is:

The voice encoder generates input codec tokens and passes them to thinker. Thinker completes prefill and obtains the last hidden states. Up to this point, it is consistent with the current mainstream design. However, I will send the last hidden states to talker and start decoding codec token immediately, so that the process of thinker decoding text token and talker decoding codec token will overlap. And as per my expectation, talker decode codec token will be faster than thinker decode text token. In this way, you can often use the codec token generated by talker to forcibly verify the text token generated by thinker.This design, which I would call cross-modal speculative decoding, seems to be better from the perspective of optimizing TTFT (or time to first voice). Of course, I'm not sure if anyone has tried to design and train a model like this. The model architecture design I described may have been tried, but it may not be trainable, so no one does it. However, I am really thinking seriously about how to optimize the reasoning efficiency of this system, even though the training and engineering implementation may be difficult.

These ideas have not been profiled at all. There are several drawbacks to consider:

1. Difficult to train: During the training phase, if Talker and Thinker are allowed to sample from Hidden States independently, Thinker's loss function needs to take into account the penalty of rollback after verification with Talker's codec token. The loss function of Talker, or the training target, will change greatly. The original training goal of receiving both hidden states and text tokens is probably "to restore the most natural acoustic features for a given word", while the model that only uses hidden states will become "to restore the ideas in the mind to the most natural acoustic features". I feel that the training target is more difficult, but now that we are pursuing e2e training, it may not be as difficult as I thought.

2. Subtitle rollback: If Talker has output a considerable length of audio, and Thinker detects a verification inconsistency, it can only roll back the text output, which will affect the user experience to a certain extent. Of course, it is much better than rolling back audio.

3. Engineering implementation: Obviously, considering the difficulty of implementing speculative sampling itself, the difficulty of rolling back subtitles is definitely not low.
